DESCRIPTION (provided by applicant): Despite advancements in drug development, the failure rate 
of new therapeutics remains high due to the translational gap between preclinical models and clinical 
outcomes. This gap delays the development of effective therapies, ultimately limiting the availability of 
life-saving treatments. Traditional 2D cultures and animal models lack pathophysiological relevance, 
while current 3D models are inefficient for drug screening applications under certain scenarios. 
Bioprinting has the potential to overcome these challenges but is constrained by short tissue shelf-life 
and logistical hurdles. This Phase I project aims to leverage Criocore’s unique cryobioprinting 
technology, which enables the fabrication of shelf-ready, long-term storable tissues, to develop a high-
throughput system tailored for preclinical drug testing of pancreatic tumor models. Specifically, an 
advanced cooling plate will be engineered for precise thermal control allowing integration with standard 
well plates, translating the technology from individual tissue production to scalable 96-well plate 
configurations. Computational fluid dynamics modeling will be employed to optimize system design, 
while real-time monitoring will ensure uniform cryopreservation across all cryobioprinted tissues. 
Additionally, bioink formulations will be refined to maintain cell functionality specifically accommodated 
to improve throughput, while optimizing cryobioprinting parameters to ensure reproducibility across 
wells. By addressing key limitations of conventional bioprinted tissues, this project will establish a high-
throughput platform for generating pathologically relevant, shelf-ready cryogenically stored 3D 
pancreatic tumor models that integrate seamlessly into drug development pipelines. The successful 
implementation of this technology will accelerate drug discovery, enhance preclinical testing reliability, 
and provide a standardized, high-throughput alternative to traditional in vitro and animal models.
PUBLIC HEALTH RELEVANCE: Developing novel preclinical in vitro models is fundamental to 
improving drug discovery, reducing failure rates, and accelerating the development of effective 
therapeutics. This project will enable high-throughput production of reproducible, pathologically relevant 
3D cryobioprinted pancreatic tumor models that can be stored long-term and used on demand. By 
providing shelf-ready tissues that better replicate human biology, this work will enhance drug testing 
efficiency, improve predictive accuracy, and support the transition away from reliance on 2D and animal 
models, ultimately advancing public health outcomes.
CRITIQUE 1
Significance: 6
Investigator(s): 4
Innovation: 4
Approach: 5
Environment: 2
Overall Impact: This proposal presents a technically promising platform for high-throughput 
cryobioprinting of pancreatic tumor models, with potential to enhance scalability and storage in 
preclinical drug screening. The integration of a cold plate with 96-well compatibility is innovative and 
supported by solid preliminary data. However, the biological scope of Phase I is narrow, with limited 
tumor complexity and unclear added value over existing 3D models. The lack of clarity on what 
structures are being printed and why bioprinting is essential weakens the rationale. Without 
benchmarking or a disease expert in pancreatic cancer, the translational relevance and near-term 
impact are limited. Overall, the platform shows potential but would benefit from clearer biological goals 
and validation pathways.

1. Significance: 
Strengths 
•The proposal for developing a high-throughput cryobioprinting platform for pancreatic tumor 
tissue models is a compelling and innovative approach to address critical limitations in 
preclinical drug development.
•If successful, the cryobioprinting platform could transform preclinical drug development by 
providing scalable, shelf-ready pancreatic tumor models compatible with HTS. This would 
accelerate drug discovery and improve clinical translation for pancreatic cancer.
Weaknesses 
•Phase I biological scope is too narrow: The proposal states that a fully developed tumor model 
is beyond Phase I, but without intermediate biological goals, it's unclear how this stage informs 
future development.
•While pancreatic cancer is a critical target, the proposal’s exclusive focus on this model may 
limit its perceived broader applicability. In addition, the system prints only pancreatic tumor cells 
without complex tissue architecture or multiple compartments, limiting the biological relevance.
•The design lacks detail on the actual 3D architecture of the printed constructs - what structures 
are being printed and why they matter biologically is not specified. Are they replicating glandular 
structures? Tumor-stroma interfaces? Just blobs in wells? This makes it hard to evaluate the 
added value of bioprinting.
2. Investigator(s): 
Strengths 
•The proximity of Criocore to MIT’s The Engine and Dr. Zhang’s laboratory, combined with the 
PI’s expertise in bioprinter development and Dr. Zhang’s leadership in biofabrication, enhances 
feasibility.
Weaknesses 
•The absence of a pancreatic cancer expert on the team raises concerns about the depth of 
disease-specific insight and the validity of the tumor modeling approach.
3. Innovation: 
Strengths 
•Automation and throughput; integration with 96-well format.
•Cryobioprinting concept itself is novel.
Weaknesses 
•Unclear what's new biologically.
•As a novel technology, cryobioprinting lacks established benchmarks, which may raise concerns 
about validation and adoption by conservative pharmaceutical industries.
•The proposal doesn’t demonstrate that their approach produces more physiologically relevant 
pancreatic tumor models than existing methods like organoids or organ-on-chip.
•The real innovative application (e.g., tumor model function in drug testing) is deferred to Phase 
II, limiting near-term impact.

1R41CA310266-014ZRG1 BBBT-F (10)
GARCIAMENDEZ, C 
4. Approach: 
Strengths 
•The use of 3D cryobioprinting integrated with a cold plate enables batch fabrication and long-
term storage of tissue models in a 96-well format, potentially addressing major bottlenecks in 
throughput and reproducibility for preclinical drug testing.
•Preliminary data show preserved cell viability and post-thaw function across multiple cell types, 
lending technical credibility to the platform’s underlying approach.
Weaknesses 
•It remains unclear why bioprinting is essential here, as many drug-screening platforms use cast 
or self-assembled 3D cultures effectively.
•The design does not define what specific tissue structures are being bioprinted (e.g., layered 
epithelium, ductal structures), making it difficult to assess whether spatial control is even 
necessary or used.
•There’s no clear standard to validate the printed tissues against real tumor tissue or existing in 
vitro models. No benchmarking is done against spheroids or organoids to show improved 
functionality or relevance.
5. Environment: 
Strengths 
•Appropriate for the project.
Weaknesses 
•None noted.

Overall Impact: The proposal lacks clear relevance to a real-world application and needs a better 
workflow.
1. Significance: 
Strengths 
•Cryopreservation of cell clusters, tumor cells while printing into a 3D construct is an important 
modality for creating workflows suitable for HTS applications in a pharmaceutical company 
workflow. 
Weaknesses 
•While it has been shown to preserve to ~80% over three months, is 3 months sufficient to be 
commercially relevant? 
•The relevance of 3 months to a proposed commercial application is unsupported.
2. Investigator(s): 
Strengths 
•Depth of expertise and capable well-functioning team across both the business and university 
groups.
Weaknesses 
•None noted.
3. Innovation: 
Strengths 
•Developing a range of bio ink formulations suitable for cryo printing a range of cells and cell 
cluster types is novel and has commercial potential.
Weaknesses 
•Again the relevance to a real-world application and workflow would improve my enthusiasm for 
this approach as a clear market pull would be evident. It isn't clear that 3 months is sufficient to 
be useful in a commercial setting. data supporting that would help.

GARCIAMENDEZ, C 
4. Approach: 
Strengths 
•The approach for developing the bio-inks and the 96 well cryopreservation fixture is well-
thought-out with well-defined milestones. 
Weaknesses 
•Are the materials and geometries optimal for any cryopreservation fixture method? 96 well 
plates are not typically flat or designed for use at -20C. The issues with uniform temperature in 
the plastic plate vs the fixture is at the interface between the two, and this was not sufficiently 
addressed. For the purpose of developing bio-inks and showing long-term functional 
performance of the tissues, 96 wells isn't required. In fact, unless it's for a HTS application, 96 
well plate may not be required. More effort in understanding where these tissues would fit in a 
workflow would help narrow the scope and improve the ability to verify a proof of concept 
system.

Overall Impact: The authors seek to scale up their technique for making 3D pancreatic tumor models 
for drug discovery and development applications.  The proposed project would likely represent a viable 
commercial product either as a research tool or platform for generating frozen tissue plates for sale to 
well-capitalized pharma customers.  The authors have strong preliminary data and a clear plan for 
scaling up the technology.  The authors reliance on iterative design of the freezing plate does raise 
timeline/budget risks if they cannot get it to work within their specifications in a few iterations.  Some 
preliminary simulations supporting the leading design would strengthen the proposal.  Additionally, a 
clinical or Pharma subject matter expert on team would ensure that the product serves market needs 
and ensures appropriate quality metrics are met by the proposed product.  
1. Significance: 
Strengths 
•The proposed project addresses an area of critical need in the therapeutic development 
space.  Time and cost required to develop new drugs is exploding and methods to reduce these 
burdens are needed allow treatments to reach patients.
•The prior research from this group is very strong.  They are leaders within the space. 
•Successful completion of this project will demonstrate a high throughput method for producing 
3D tissues that can integrate into automated drug screening workflows.  The proposed project 
would likely represent a viable commercial product either as a research tool or platform for 
generating frozen tissue plates for sale to well-capitalized Pharma customers.  
•IP is in place (although license contingent on receiving this grant)
Weaknesses 
•While the technical rationale for pursuing pancreatic cancer as a first demonstration platform is 
clear based on tissue properties, the business case for pursuing this cell type is not justified 
within the proposal. 
•A discussion of how the proposed solution integrates automated screening platforms would be 
helpful to justify that their solution meets the needs of this space. 
2. Investigator(s): 
Strengths 
•The PI has sufficient experience within the space and has pursued additional business 
training.  Funding for BD consultants will help support his idea's development.  
Weaknesses

Weaknesses

reproducible constructs." It would be better if they could provide a DoE showing how they will 
map parameter space to optimize this process.  Iterative tuning may find local optimum 
conditions but miss global optimum.  
•Some technical risk on temperature uniformity based on fluid flow through the block.  Fluid may 
change temperature from inlet/outlet unless flow rate is maintained at high level and fluid 
reservoir is large.
