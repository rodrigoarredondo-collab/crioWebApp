Title: Development of a Cryobioprinted Pancreatic Tumor Model for High-Throughput Preclinical Testing

A. Significance

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains one of the deadliest cancers, with a five-year survival rate below 12%. Current preclinical models fail to recapitulate the complex tumor microenvironment, leading to poor translation from bench to bedside. Two-dimensional cell culture systems cannot model tumor heterogeneity, stromal interactions, or the dense fibrotic desmoplastic reaction characteristic of PDAC. Animal models, while more physiologically relevant, are costly, time-consuming, and raise ethical concerns.

Three-dimensional bioprinted tumor models offer a promising middle ground. By precisely depositing cells and biomaterials in predefined architectures, bioprinting enables the creation of tissues that better mimic in vivo complexity. However, a major bottleneck exists: once printed, these tissues must be used immediately or they degrade, limiting their utility for high-throughput screening and multi-site studies.

Our project addresses this critical gap by developing a cryobioprinting platform that integrates cryopreservation directly into the bioprinting process. This approach yields tissues that can be stored, shipped, and thawed on demand, fundamentally changing the logistics of 3D tumor model deployment.

B. Investigator(s)

The Principal Investigator (PI), Dr. Jane Smith, has over 15 years of experience in bioprinting and tissue engineering. She has published over 80 peer-reviewed articles and holds 5 patents in bioprinting technologies. The research team includes two postdoctoral researchers with expertise in hydrogel chemistry and cell biology.

C. Innovation

Our cryobioprinting approach is unique in the field. Unlike conventional bioprinting followed by separate cryopreservation, our system freezes the tissue in situ during the printing process itself. The bioink formulation incorporates cryoprotective agents (CPAs) at optimized concentrations, enabling cell survival through the freeze-thaw cycle. We selected CPA concentrations of 5%, 10%, and 15% DMSO based on preliminary viability screening, though a comprehensive dose-response study has not yet been performed.

We believe our bioink is unique and superior to commercially available alternatives because it combines a gelatin-alginate matrix with our proprietary CPA blend, achieving over 85% post-thaw viability.

Similar in situ freezing concepts have been explored in the literature, but our approach differs in its integration of real-time temperature monitoring and adaptive extrusion control.

D. Approach

Aim 1: Optimize the cryobioprinting protocol for pancreatic tumor constructs.
We will systematically vary bioink composition, printing parameters, and freezing rates to identify conditions that maximize post-thaw cell viability and structural integrity. Success will be evaluated using live/dead assays, mechanical testing, and histological analysis.

Aim 2: Develop a high-throughput cryobioprinting system.
Building on the optimized protocol from Aim 1, we will scale up to a multi-nozzle system capable of producing 96 constructs per run. We plan to complete this optimization within 6 months. Statistical comparisons will be made using ANOVA with post-hoc testing, though specific sample sizes have not yet been determined through formal power analysis. Batch-to-batch variability will be monitored but we have not yet defined a formal quality control protocol for bioink composition.

Aim 3: Validate the cryobioprinted pancreatic tumor model.
We will compare our model against traditional 2D cultures and fresh bioprinted tissues using drug response assays, gene expression profiling, and proteomic analysis. Future work may include comparison with patient-derived xenograft models, though this is beyond the scope of the current proposal.
